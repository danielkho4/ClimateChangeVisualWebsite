Here we can see a correlation between the temperature anomalies and the carbon emmisions from 1980-2016. They are both steadily increasing and while correlation does not equal causation, we have to think about the issue of CO2 levels and climate change. The spike in warming from 2014 to 2016 coincided with extreme weather events worldwide, including heat waves, droughts, floods, extensive melting of polar ice and global coral bleaching.


Here we can see that the United States was the largest contributor to the world's CO2 emmisions in 2016. By clicking on each node in the treemap, we can break down the categories to further years and see how levels have changed over time for each specific country.

Next we will investigate the cause these rising CO2 and temperature levels have on the world's glaciers.


As we can see, the area of Glacier data is steadily decreasing at higher and higher rates. The cumulative mass lost is getting larger as the years go by, just as temperatures levels and CO2 emissions rise. We see a clear correlation between these three metrics.
